My conversation with Dr. Baker, a professor in public health at Elon University, framed public health as inherently interdisciplinary, drawing from fields such as sociology, environmental science, and epidemiology to better understand health at the community and population level. Rather than focusing solely on individual outcomes, public health is interested in the broader systems, like policy, that shape health experiences. 

A key takeaway from this conversation was the importance of collaboration and community-based approaches. Public health research often uses participatory methods that aim to redistribute power between researchers and community members, encouraging cultural awareness. This aligns with Holzman’s ideas on an engaged art history in that both prioritize lived experience and shared knowledge production. 

Dr. Baker also worked on a dance film alongside fellow Elon professor Keisha Wall. This creative practice addressed the Black maternal health crisis and is available on YouTube for viewers today. [link here] This film demonstrated how artistic expression can expand the reach of scholarship, making issues like the Black maternal health crisis more visible for wider audiences. Dr. Baker emphasized the critical role that public health plays in reproductive justice by bringing a multi-level perspective to advocacy. It challenges the tendency to place responsibility on individuals, instead highlighting systemic factors and including public health expertise in the conversations.
